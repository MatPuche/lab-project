Lab project:

COUNT EVERY PERSON IN THE WORLD


The aim of this project is to assess the accuracy and reliability of two datasets depicting the world population and its spatial distribution in 2015. We particularly focused on the tiles of group 9, which correspond to the East part of the world (China, Japan, North and South Korea and some parts of Mongolia and Russia).


GHS-POP

The first dataset to be analysed is Global Human Settlement - Population Grid, year 2015 (GHS-POP), a spatial raster produced by the Joint Research Centre (i.e. the European Commission's science and knowledge service), downloadable on https://ghsl.jrc.ec.europa.eu/data.php. It represents the distribution of population in 2015, expressed as the number of people per cell.

To produce these data, they extracted from satellite imagery the build-up areas and mixed it with the census data, which resulted in a layer of 1km of resolution providing the number of unhabitants per cell.


WorldPop

The second dataset to be analysed is WorldPop - Population Counts, Unconstrained global mosaics 2000/2020, year 2015, a spatial raster resulting from the WorldPop project led at the University of Southampton, downladable on www.worldpop.org. It estimates the human population distribution in 2015, expressed here again as the number of people per cell.

To produce these data, they used the population and house censuses and desaggregated them to grid cell-based counts. Unlike GHS-POP, they decided not to use the building and residential settlement datasets as a mask because they consider that no settlement dataset is accurate enough to identify all the build-up areas. This is the major difference between the two methods, which results in differences in the distribution of population on the two maps as we will see in the two map analysis sections.


Intercomparison

In this section, we proceed to a comparison of the 2 datasets to assess how much they match each other. It brings out a first idea of their accuracy and enables to understand their differences.
